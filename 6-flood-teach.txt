  Version C: "The Impossibility Stack" — Full Design

  The Opening Problem

  Pre-flood, not post-hurricane. Here's why: post-hurricane you already know who flooded. Pre-flood is the decision problem — you have 72
   hours, finite cots/boats/staff, and a geospatial foundation model ranking ZCTAs by flood vulnerability. R² = 0.73. Which rankings do
  you act on?

  That question has a precise logical structure. It is asking: for which ZCTAs does the model's prediction carry enough structural
  integrity to license a resource allocation decision? That is asking for a certificate, not a score.

  ---
  The Impossibility Stack (Citation Arc)

  Each rung adds exactly one primitive. This is the spine of the paper.

  Rung 0 — The scalar baseline
  ▎ R² tells you how much variance the model explains, on average, across all ZCTAs.

  The pre-flood manager asks: "Great — but which individual ZCTAs are reliable?" R² is silent. Stein (1956) formalized why: in ≥3
  dimensions, the least-squares estimator is inadmissible. Individual predictions from a high-R² model can be arbitrarily wrong even when
   the aggregate is good. The scalar compresses distributional information you need for individual decisions.

  What's missing: per-location uncertainty that doesn't average away.

  Rung 1 — Bilodeau 2024: No complete linear attribution
  You try to explain the model's ZCTA predictions using feature attributions (SHAP). Bilodeau proves that no attribution method that
  satisfies completeness and linearity can detect whether a prediction came from genuine flood-signal or from an artifactual correlation
  (e.g., poverty correlated with both flood risk and flood exposure to reporting gaps). You cannot distinguish "model sees flood
  gradient" from "model sees a socioeconomic proxy" using attribution alone.

  What's missing: a measure of signal purity — how much of R is genuine flood signal vs. noise/proxy. That is α.

  Rung 2 — Goldberger 2005 / NCA lineage: Compatibility is not derivable from accuracy
  Your signal-pure embedding (high α) can still fail the flood manager. If the embedding space doesn't geometrically align with the
  solver's decision boundary — e.g., elevation gradients are present in the features but the solver's principal components are oriented
  toward population density — you can have clean signal that is geometrically inaccessible. Frosst (2019) formalizes this as neighbor
  entanglement: the right labels are present in the training set, but the geometry forces the solver to retrieve the wrong ones.
  Goldberger proves the fix requires a separate neighborhood-formation measure, not a transformation of accuracy.

  What's missing: a measure of geometric alignment between the embedding and the solver's decision structure. That is κ.

  Rung 3 — McClean et al. 2018 (barren plateaus) / stability literature
  Pre-flood, your model ingests three modal sources simultaneously: FEMA flood zones (structural), CDC Social Vulnerability Index
  (socioeconomic), and USGS elevation derivatives (terrain). These sources can agree or disagree. When they disagree on a ZCTA, the
  model's prediction is a weighted average of contradictory signals. It can appear confident (low variance) while being structurally
  unstable — if any one modal source is updated (e.g., a levee failure changes the flood zone classification), the prediction flips.
  McClean shows that learning in high-gradient-variance regimes (barren plateaus in optimization; modal incoherence in inference)
  produces formally correct solutions that are dynamically fragile. Sudjianto (2026) instantiates this at inference time: Δy (local label
   dispersion) is invisible to SHAP but visible as modal scatter.

  What's missing: a measure of cross-modal stability — how scattered κᵢ values are across the M modal sources. That is σ.

  Rung 4 — The N-ceiling: what is the theoretical prediction limit for this ZCTA?
  After assembling (α, κ, σ), the flood manager still needs to know: is this task fundamentally hard, or is the embedding failing at
  something achievable? N-ceiling(t) = 1 − max_k(R²_k(t)) is the irreducible floor — the best any embedding achieves. A ZCTA near a bayou
   in a tile of heterogeneous development has a high N-ceiling: the underlying dynamics are not well-captured by ZCTA-aggregated features
   at any granularity. The model is not failing; the task is underdetermined at this resolution. That is a different action (escalate to
  sub-ZCTA data) than a low-N-ceiling ZCTA where the model is failing a tractable task (try a better embedding).

  What's added: actionable diagnosis — is this a representation failure or a task-resolution failure?

  The stack arrives at GeoRSCT: The certificate (α, κ, σ, N_ceiling) is what you get when you supply exactly the primitive each
  impossibility requires. Nothing more. Nothing less.

  ---
  The Three Creative Experiments

  Experiment 1 — The Invariance Test (adiabatic probe)
  Physics framing: a good representation should preserve conserved quantities under slow transformations.

  Take the same ZCTAs observed across three temporal snapshots: 2018 (pre-Florence), 2019 (post-Florence recovery), 2020 (pre-Isaias). A
  representation with high κ should produce certificates that track flood-risk changes smoothly — the κ trajectory is continuous. A low-κ
   embedding will show discontinuous certificate jumps at event boundaries, not because the underlying risk changed, but because the
  representation is geometrically fragile.

  Teaching point: κ is a trajectory property. Measuring it once is insufficient. The temporal derivative dκ/dt is a new diagnostic — an
  embedding that produces smooth κ trajectories is more trustworthy for predictive alerting than one that jumps.

  This directly exercises σ vs κ independence: at the event boundary, σ spikes (modal sources temporarily disagree during the event)
  while κ can remain stable if the geometry is sound. That is an off-diagonal cell in the (κ, σ) plane — exactly what the current
  benchmark doesn't generate.

  Experiment 2 — The Modal Coherence Ablation
  Physics framing: if three instruments disagree, which one do you trust? The answer is the one whose withdrawal collapses σ toward 0.

  For each ZCTA in the flood dataset, compute the certificate under three embedding configurations:
  - Full: all three modal sources (FEMA zones + SVI + elevation)
  - Ablation A: drop FEMA zones → certificate_A
  - Ablation B: drop SVI → certificate_B
  - Ablation C: drop elevation → certificate_C

  Define modal load = |σ_full − σ_ablation|. The modal source with highest load is the one causing incoherence.

  Teaching point: this gives σ a causal interpretation — not just "the modalities disagree" but "modality X is the source of
  disagreement." This is the GeoRSCT analog of Sudjianto's R_j (neighborhood-formation score) — which features explain why these
  neighbors were retrieved. For flood management: if elevation removal collapses σ but FEMA-zones removal doesn't, the terrain data is
  incoherent with the socioeconomic model. The correct intervention is physical, not statistical.

  Experiment 3 — The N-ceiling Decision Boundary
  Physics framing: the Pareto frontier separates tasks where better embeddings help from tasks where they don't.

  The existing GeoRSCT data shows N-ceiling(elevation) = 0.319 on the clean environmental substrate and 0.527 on the full 27-task suite.
  For flood ZCTAs, compute N-ceiling per ZCTA-type and map it onto the physical domain:

  - ZCTAs with N-ceiling < 0.2 → task is tractable; current embeddings underperform the achievable ceiling → representation problem
  - ZCTAs with N-ceiling 0.2–0.5 → moderate difficulty; embedding choice matters significantly
  - ZCTAs with N-ceiling > 0.5 → task is fundamentally hard at ZCTA granularity; the information doesn't exist at this resolution →
  resolution problem

  Teaching point: the N-ceiling separates two entirely different interventions. "Deploy a better embedding" is the right answer for the
  first group. "Disaggregate to census tract or parcel level" is the right answer for the third. A scalar R² cannot tell you which
  intervention to pursue — it conflates representation failure and task-resolution failure into the same number.

  ---
  The Teaching Arc (Three 90-minute sessions)

  Session 1 — "Why a score is not an answer"
  Start with the 72-hour pre-flood decision. Give students a model with R² = 0.73 and a ranked list of 500 ZCTAs. Ask them to identify
  the top 50 for resource pre-positioning. Let them try. Watch them fail — they have no basis for distinguishing reliable from unreliable
   predictions. Then walk the impossibility stack. Each rung is a published result. End: students derive what certificate they need from
  first principles, without being told.

  Session 2 — "Reading a certificate"
  Give students the (α, κ, σ, N_ceiling) tuples for 30 specific ZCTAs with known flood outcomes. Can they classify each into the four
  failure modes? Can they identify which ZCTAs a flood manager should act on? The goal: students internalize that the certificate is a
  decision instrument, not a description.

  Session 3 — "Designing an experiment that exercises the space"
  Present the finding that GeoRSCT's current benchmark doesn't span [0,1]³. Ask students: what ZCTA types, data sources, or constructed
  scenarios would force each off-diagonal cell? Let them design Experiments 1–3 themselves. The flood problem is the scaffolding; the
  certificate space coverage is the insight they're meant to arrive at independently.
