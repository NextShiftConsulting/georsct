  Key results from the (embedding × task) turbulence surface:

  [1] Embedding explains turbulence far more than it explains R²

  ┌────────────────────┬───────────┬──────────────────┐
  │       Source       │ η² for r2 │ η² for rsct_turb │
  ├────────────────────┼───────────┼──────────────────┤
  │ Embedding          │ 0.281     │ 0.634            │
  ├────────────────────┼───────────┼──────────────────┤
  │ Solver             │ 0.074     │ 0.003            │
  ├────────────────────┼───────────┼──────────────────┤
  │ Embedding × Solver │ 0.033     │ 0.132            │
  └────────────────────┴───────────┴──────────────────┘

  Trajectory choice (embedding) is the dominant driver of turbulence — more so than for predictive quality. Solver is
  nearly irrelevant for turbulence.

  [2] Within each geometry, embedding explains 79–82% of turbulence variance. This confirms the
  trajectory-within-geometry reading is real, not noise.

  [3] Geometry-conditional laminarity (η²_interaction = 0.132). geo_spatial is the clearest case: most turbulent on
  elevation (0.415) but only rank 6 of 7 on diabetes (0.380). It fits the geographic gradient geometry specifically.
  gnn_v2 is consistently turbulent across all three geometries — a trajectory that never settles into laminar flow.

  [4] Better representations are MORE turbulent (rho(turb, r2) = +0.615). The higher-fidelity embeddings are
  geometrically selective — they distinguish easy ZCTAs from hard ones sharply, widening the D*/D per-sample
  distribution. Turbulence is measuring selectivity, not noise. noisy_control is the most laminar (uniformly mediocre
  D*/D across all ZCTAs).
